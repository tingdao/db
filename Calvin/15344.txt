<div class="comment-content"><p><strong>17.</strong><em>For thou art the glory of their strength. </em>The same sentiment is confirmed when it is declared, that God never leaves his faithful servants destitute of strength. By the appellation <em>the glory of their strength, </em>which is ascribed to him, is meant that they are always so sustained by his present aid as to have just ground to glory in him; or which amounts to the same thing, that his power appears always glorious in aiding and sustaining them. They are, however, at the same time, reminded of the duty of yielding to God all the praise of their being preserved in safety. If this is true as to the present life, it is much more truly applicable to the spiritual life of the soul. Farther, the more highly to magnify this instance of God’s liberality, we are taught, at the same time, that it depends entirely upon his good pleasure, there being no other cause of it. <span class="cite-ref">(536)</span> Whence it follows, that they are wholly bound and indebted to Him who is induced by his free bounty alone to continue to extend to them his help. </p><div class="notes"><p><span class="cite-note">(536)</span> “The Hebrew <span class="lang he-il">ליהוה</span>, must be rendered <em>of </em>or <em>from the Lord</em>, in both places in this verse: ‘Of the Lord is our shield <em>or </em>defense;’ ‘Of the Lord, <em>or </em>from him,’ <em>i e</em>. , of his appointment, ‘is our King.’”—<em>Hammond </em></p></div></div>
