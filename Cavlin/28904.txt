<div class="comment-content"><p><em>Knowledge </em>may be taken in two senses — either as meaning doctrine itself, or skill in acting properly and <em>knowingly. </em>The latter appears to me the more likely, as he immediately adds — <em>the word of truth </em>The <em>Spirit </em>is taken by <em>metonymy, </em>to denote spiritual graces. Frivolous, however, is the cavil of Chrysostom, who infers from this, that the other excellences are peculiar to the Apostle, because he makes mention of the <em>Spirit </em>separately, as if <em>kindness, knowledge, pureness, armor of righteousness, </em>were from any other source, than from the Holy Spirit. He makes mention, however, of the Spirit separately, as a general term in the midst of particular instances. <span class="cite-ref">(592)</span> The <em>power of God </em>showed itself in many things — in magnanimity, in efficacy in the maintaining of the truth, in the propagation of the Gospel, in victory over enemies, and the like. </p><div class="notes"><p><span class="cite-note">(592)</span> “<span class="lang el-gr">᾿Εν πνεύματι ἁγίω</span> — ’In demonstration of the <em>Holy Spirit </em>— so that I showed that the Holy Spirit wrought by me.’ It is possible, that in these words, Paul makes an allusion to the <span class="lang el-gr">χαρίσματα</span>, (<em>gifts</em>,) but it seems better, nevertheless, to suppose with Calvin, that he sets <em>genus </em> and <em>species </em> over against each other.” — <em>Billroth</em>. — <em>Ed. </em></p></div></div>
