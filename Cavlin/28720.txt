<div class="comment-content"><p><strong>2.</strong><em>If you keep in memory </em>—<em>unless in vain </em><span class="cite-ref">(9)</span> These two expressions are very cutting. In the <em>first, </em>he reproves their carelessness or fickleness, because such a sudden fall was an evidence that they had never understood what had been delivered to them, or that their knowledge of it had been loose and floating, inasmuch as it had so quickly vanished. By the <em>second, </em>he warns them that they had needlessly and uselessly professed allegiance to Christ, if they did not hold fast this main doctrine. <span class="cite-ref">(10)</span></p><div class="notes"><p><span class="cite-note">(9)</span> “Our version does not express intelligibly the sense of <span class="lang el-gr">ἐκτὸς εἰ μὴ εἰκὢ ἐπιστεύσατε</span> by rendering it so <em>literally </em>— <em>unless ye have believed in vain. To believe in vain, </em>according to the use of ancient languages, is to believe <em>without just reason and authority, </em>giving credit to idle reports as true and authentic. Thus <em>Plutarch, </em>speaking of some story which passed current, says, <span class="lang el-gr">τοῦτο ἡμεῖς ἐ᾿ἴπομεν ἐν τί τῶν εἰκὢ πεπιστεύμενων</span> — “this I said was one of those tales which are believed without any good authority.” (Sympos. lib. 1, quaest. 6.) The Latins used <em>credere frustra </em> — <em>to believe in vain, </em>or <em>temere </em> — <em>(rashly.) Kypke </em>takes notice that <span class="lang el-gr">ἐκτὸς εἰ μὴ</span>, for <em>except </em>or <em>unless, </em>which has long been a suspected phrase, is used more than ten times by <em>Lucian. </em>It is also used by <em>Plutarch </em>in the Life of Demosthenes, volume 4.” — <em>Alexander’s Paraphrase </em>on <span class="bvs">1Co_15:0</span>. (London, 1766,) — <em>Ed. </em></p><p><span class="cite-note">(10)</span> “<em class="lang fr-fr">Ce </em><span class="lang fr-fr">principal poinct de la foy</span><em>; </em>” — “This main article of faith.” </p></div></div>
