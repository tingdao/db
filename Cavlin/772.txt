<div class="comment-content"><p><strong>44.</strong><em>And tarry with him a few days</em>. This circumstance mitigates the severity of banishment. For the shortness of the time of suffering avails not a little to support us in adversity. And it was probable that the enmity of Esau would not prove so obstinate as to be unassuaged by his brother’s absence. In the Hebrew expression which is translated “a few days,” the word few is literally “one” put in the plural number. <span class="cite-ref">(51)</span> Rebekah means, that as soon as Jacob should have gone away of his own accord, the memory of the offense would be obliterated from the mind of Esau; as if she had said, Only depart hence for a little while, and we shall soon assuage his anger. </p><div class="notes"><p><span class="cite-note">(51)</span>Hebraice ad verbum habetur, Unis diebus<span class="lang he-il">ימים אחדים</span> (<em>yamim achedim</em>). There is no mode of giving a literal rendering of the expression in the English language. — <em>Ed </em></p></div></div>
