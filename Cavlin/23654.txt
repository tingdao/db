<div class="comment-content"><p><em>And pollute the man. </em>Instead of the verb <em>pollute, </em>the Greek term is <span class="lang el-gr">κοινοῖ</span>, <em>make common; </em>as Mark, a little before, ( <span class="bvs">Mar_7:2</span>,) used the phrase, <span class="lang el-gr">κοιναῖς χερσὶ</span>, <em>with </em>common <em>hands, </em>for <em>with </em>unclean <em>hands. </em><span class="cite-ref">(412)</span> It is a Hebrew phrase; <span class="cite-ref">(413)</span> for, since God had set apart the Jews on the condition that they should separate themselves from all the pollutions of the Gentiles, everything that was inconsistent with this holiness was called <em>common, </em>that is, <em>profane. </em></p><p><strong>(412)</strong> “<em class="lang fr-fr">Les mains communes </em><span class="lang fr-fr">pour </span><em class="lang fr-fr">souillees </em><span class="lang fr-fr">et </span><em class="lang fr-fr">non lavees </em><em>; </em>” — “<em>common hands </em>for <em>polluted </em>and <em>not washed.” </em></p><p><strong>(413)</strong> “<span class="lang fr-fr">C’est une facon de parler propre aux Hebrieux</span>;” — “it is a mode of speaking peculiar to the Hebrews.” </p></div>
