<div class="comment-content"><p><strong>15.</strong><em>I will pray with the spirit </em>Lest any one should ask, by way of objection, <em>“Will </em>the spirit then be useless in prayer?” he teaches, that it is lawful, indeed, to <em>pray with the spirit, </em>provided the mind be at the same time employed, that is, the <em>understanding </em>He allows, therefore, and sanctions the use of a spiritual gift in prayer, but requires, what is the main thing, that the mind be not unemployed. <span class="cite-ref">(834)</span></p><p>When he says, I <em>will sing Psalms, </em>or, I <em>will sing, </em>he makes use of a particular instance, instead of a general statement. For, as the praises of God were the subject-matter of the Psalms, he means by <em>the singing of Psalms </em><span class="cite-ref">(835)</span> — <em>blessing God, </em>or <em>rendering thanks </em>to him, for in our supplications, we either ask something from God, or we acknowledge some blessing that has been conferred upon us. From this passage, however, we at the same time infer, that the custom of singing was, even at that time, in use among believers, as appears, also, from Pliny, who, writing at least forty years, or thereabouts, after the death of Paul, mentions, that the Christians were accustomed to sing Psalms to Christ before day-break. <span class="cite-ref">(836)</span> I have also no doubt, that, from the very first, they followed the custom of the Jewish Church in singing Psalms. </p><div class="notes"><p><span class="cite-note">(834)</span> “<span class="lang fr-fr">Que ne soit point sans intelligence</span>;” — “That it be not without understanding.” </p><p><span class="cite-note">(835)</span> The original word is <span class="lang el-gr">ψαλῶ</span> — <em>I will sing Psalms </em>It is the same verb that is made use of by <em>James</em>, (<span class="bvs">Jas_5:13</span>,) <span class="lang el-gr">εὐθυμεῖ τίς; ψαλλέτω</span> — <em>Is any one cheerful</em>: <em>let him sing Psalms</em>. — <em>Ed </em></p><p><span class="cite-note">(836)</span> Pliny’s letter, referred to by Calvin, (written A.D. 107,) is given at full length (as translated by Dr. Lardner) in Horne’s Introduction, volume 1. — <em>Ed</em>. </p></div></div>
