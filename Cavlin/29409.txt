<div class="comment-content"><p><strong>18</strong><em>Rejoice ye</em>. By the alacrity which he thus discovers, he encourages the Philippians, and enkindles in them a desire to meet death with firmness, <span class="cite-ref">(142)</span> inasmuch as believers suffer no harm from it. For he has formerly taught them that death would be <em>gain </em>to himself, (<span class="bvs">Phi_1:21</span>;) here, on the other hand, he is chiefly concerned that his death may not disconcert the Philippians. <span class="cite-ref">(143)</span> He, accordingly, declares that it is no ground of sorrow; nay, that they have occasion of joy, inasmuch as they will find it to be productive of advantage. For, although it was in itself a serious loss to be deprived of such a teacher, it was no slight compensation that the gospel was confirmed by his blood. In the mean time, he lets them know that to himself personally death would be matter of joy. The rendering of Erasmus, taking it in the present tense, <em>Ye rejoice</em>, is altogether unsuitable. </p><div class="notes"><p><span class="cite-note">(142)</span> “<span class="lang fr-fr">Les enflambe a mourir constamment, et receuoir la mort d’vn cœur magnanime</span>;” — “Enkindles them to die with firmness, and meet death with magnanimity.” </p><p><span class="cite-note">(143)</span> “<span class="lang fr-fr">Que sa mort ne trouble et estonne les Philippians</span>;” — “That his death may not distress and alarm the Philippians.” </p></div></div>
