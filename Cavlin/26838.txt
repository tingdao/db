<div class="comment-content"><p><strong>13.</strong><em>And sat down on the judgment-seat. </em>Hence we see what conflicting opinions passed through the mind of Pilate, as if he had been a stage-player who was acting two characters. He ascends <em>the judgment-seat, </em>in order to pronounce sentence of death on Christ solemnly, and in the customary form; <span class="cite-ref">(164)</span> and yet he declares openly, that he does so reluctantly and against his conscience. When he calls Christ <em>king, </em>he speaks ironically, meaning that it was a trivial charge which the Jews brought against him; or rather, for the purpose of allaying their fury, he warns them, that it would bring disgrace on the whole nation, if a report were spread abroad, that a person of that nation had been condemned to die for aspiring to kingly power. </p><p><em>In the place which is called the Stone-pavement, but in the Hebrew, Gabbatha. </em>When the Evangelist says, that <span class="lang he-il">גבתא</span> (<em>Gabbatha</em>) was the name of the place <em>in </em>Hebrew he means the Chaldaic or Syriac language, which was then in common use; for in <em>Hebrew, </em><span class="lang he-il">גבה</span> (<em>Gabach</em>) means <em>to be lofty. </em>It was proper, therefore, that Christ should be condemned from <em>a lofty place, </em>that he, coming from heaven as the supreme Judge, may acquit us at the last day. </p><div class="notes"><p><span class="cite-note">(164)</span> “<span class="lang fr-fr">Solennellement a la facon accoustumee</span>.” </p></div></div>
