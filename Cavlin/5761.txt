<div class="comment-content"><p><strong>2</strong><em>My doctrine shall drop as the rain. </em>Some, as I think improperly, here resolve the future tense into the optative mood, <span class="cite-ref">(248)</span> for in this splendid eulogium he rather celebrates, in order to commend his doctrine, the fruitfulness <span class="cite-ref">(249)</span> which is actually imparted to it by the Holy Spirit, than asks for it to be given to him; and my readers must at once perceive that such a request would have been by no means seasonable. He therefore compares his speech to rain or dew, as if he had said that, if only the people were like the soil in a state of softness and preparation, he would deliver doctrine to them which would irrigate them unto abundant fruitfulness. </p><p>Although this expression refers especially, and <span class="lang el-gr">κατ ᾿ ἐξοχὴν</span> to the Song, still its force and propriety extends to all divine teaching; for God never speaks except to render men fruitful in good works, just as, by instilling succulency and vigor into the earth by means of rain, He makes it fertile for the production of fruit. But, like the rocks and stones, which imbibe no moisture from the most abundant rains, so many are hindered by their own perversity from being fertilized by spiritual irrigation. Wherefore Moses indirectly throws the blame upon the Israelites, if the doctrine of this Song should drop upon them in vain. </p><div class="notes"><p><span class="cite-note">(248)</span> So the <em>LXX., V</em>. , Vatablus, Junius, and others. Ainsworth combines the two, and says, “<em>shall drop, </em>or <em>let it drop, </em>as being a wish, and also a promise, that his doctrine should be profitable and effectual,” etc. </p><p><span class="cite-note">(249)</span><em>“L’eloquence.” </em>—<em>Fr. </em></p></div></div>
